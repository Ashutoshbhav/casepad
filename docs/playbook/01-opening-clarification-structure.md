## 1. Opening — first 60 seconds

### 1.1 Standard openers (verbatim phrases real interviewers use)

1. "Our client is..." — the universal MBB opener; every published McKinsey/BCG/Bain case begins with client identification. [McKinsey Careers — Interviewing](https://www.mckinsey.com/careers/interviewing)
2. "Let me tell you my understanding of the case context and key objectives." — the play-back / "map habit" frame candidates are taught to use, and what good interviewers reward immediately. [Hacking the Case Interview — MBB Guide](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mbb-case-interview)
3. "Take a moment to think about this and walk me through how you'd approach it." — McKinsey-style standard handoff after the prompt. [IGotAnOffer — McKinsey case examples](https://igotanoffer.com/blogs/mckinsey-case-interview-blog/case-interview-examples)
4. "How would you think about this problem?" — McKinsey open-ended handoff. [Hacking the Case Interview — McKinsey Style](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mckinsey-style-case-interview)
5. "What would you want to know?" — invitation to clarifying questions, standard McKinsey opener. [IGotAnOffer — Case examples](https://igotanoffer.com/blogs/mckinsey-case-interview-blog/case-interview-examples)
6. "Why don't we start with you telling me what you'd want to investigate?" — Bain/BCG conversational variant. [Management Consulted — Case Interview Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)
7. "Feel free to take notes." — standard permission-granting opener. [PrepLounge](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/how-to-ask-questions-in-case-interview-5988)
8. "Do you have any clarifying questions before we dive in?" — explicit invitation; common at Bain. [CaseCoach — How interviews differ](https://casecoach.com/b/how-different-are-the-interviews-at-mckinsey-bain-and-bcg/)
9. "Take a minute to organize your thoughts." — standard think-time grant; 60-90 sec is the norm. [Management Consulted — Case Interview Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)
10. "I'd like to walk you through a case our team recently worked on." — Bain/BCG conversational opener; signals interviewer-led case. [CaseCoach](https://casecoach.com/b/how-different-are-the-interviews-at-mckinsey-bain-and-bcg/)
11. "Where would you start?" — pure hypothesis-prompt opener at McKinsey. [Career In Consulting — Hypothesis-driven approach](https://careerinconsulting.com/hypothesis/)

16. "Today I'd like to walk you through a problem I've been working on." — first-person Bain framing — interviewer presents the case as their own engagement. [CaseCoach](https://casecoach.com/b/how-different-are-the-interviews-at-mckinsey-bain-and-bcg/)
17. "Here's the situation." — neutral opener, leads directly into prompt. [Hacking the Case Interview — MBB Guide](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mbb-case-interview)
18. "I'm going to give you some background, then I'll ask you a question." — interviewer-led McKinsey signal that the case will be segmented. [Caseinterview.com — Interviewer-led](https://caseinterview.com/interviewer-led-case-interview)

55. "Don't dive into numbers yet — I want your structure first." — common interviewer correction at the open. [Management Consulted — Top 10 mistakes](https://managementconsulted.com/top-10-case-interview-mistakes/)

### 1.2 What good candidates do in the first 30 seconds

56. **Listen end-to-end without interrupting** the prompt before noting anything. [Hacking the Case Interview — MBB Guide](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mbb-case-interview)
57. **Pause briefly to organise thoughts** rather than starting to talk immediately.
58. **Restate the client's objective in their own words** as a one-sentence playback. [Management Consulted — Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)
59. **Flag the key constraint or context** explicitly (e.g. "5-year horizon, profitability lens").
60. **State the success metric** ("So success = grow EBITDA by X%") before structuring. [PrepLounge — Clarifying](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/clarifying-questions-1786)
61. **Ask 1-2 sharp clarifying questions, not more.** Ceiling is 2-3; more signals nervousness. [PrepLounge — How many clarifying questions](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/how-many-clarifying-questions-are-appropriate-and-when-should-they-be-asked-1869)
62. **Confirm ambiguous terminology** ("Just to confirm — by 'profitability', do we mean EBITDA or net margin?"). [Hacking the Case Interview — Clarifying](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/clarifying-questions-to-ask-in-a-case-interview)
63. **Take notes in a structured layout** (objective top, facts left, framework right). [Casebasix — Structure flow](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/case-interview-structure-flow)
64. **Echo numbers back** to confirm they heard correctly: e.g. "Great, so I think I heard all of the information correctly. There was one data point at the end that I would like to clarify. There's overall about 250,000 acres dedicated towards mango production. Is that correct?" — verbatim from a real Jenny Rae Le Roux mock. [Management Consulted — McKinsey Example](https://managementconsulted.com/mckinsey-case-interview-example-solved-by-consulting-candidate/)
65. **Frame clarifications as "tell-then-verify"** rather than open queries: "It sounds like Agrochem is pretty diversified… I want to make sure that's appropriate in my set of assumptions." — preferred to "How big is Agrochem?" [Management Consulted — McKinsey Example](https://managementconsulted.com/mckinsey-case-interview-example-solved-by-consulting-candidate/)
66. **Use 60-90 seconds for structure**, not more. [Hacking the Case Interview — Timing](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/case-interview-timing)
67. **Stay calm and quiet during think-time** — silence is fine if you're working. [Casebasix — Structure thinking](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/structure-thinking-case-interview)
68. **Open with a confident voice and posture.** Interviewers register tone before content. [Sensei AI — Bain vs BCG vs McKinsey](https://www.senseicopilot.com/blog/bain-vs-bcg-vs-mckinsey-interview-differences-explained)
69. **Verbally signpost the playback.** "Let me make sure I have this right." [Management Consulted — Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)
70. **State an early hypothesis** when invited. "My initial hunch is X, and here's why." [Career In Consulting — Hypothesis](https://careerinconsulting.com/hypothesis/)
71. **Order clarifications by importance** — business model first, then objective scope, then constraints. [Hacking the Case Interview — Clarifying](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/clarifying-questions-to-ask-in-a-case-interview)
72. **Treat ambiguity as deliberate** — assume the interviewer left things vague to see if you'd surface them. [PrepLounge — Critique of structure](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/critique-of-this-case-interview-structure-904)
73. **Recap *after* clarifying questions, not before** — clarify first, then confirm understanding. [Hacking the Case Interview — Clarifying](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/clarifying-questions-to-ask-in-a-case-interview)
74. **Say "thank you" or acknowledge** before pausing for think-time — small social cue interviewers notice. [PrepLounge](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/how-to-ask-questions-in-case-interview-5988)
75. **Write down the question verbatim** so you can refer back. [Casebasix — Structure flow](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/case-interview-structure-flow)
76. **Keep first questions short and crisp** — 1-2 sentences max each. [Hacking the Case Interview — Clarifying](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/clarifying-questions-to-ask-in-a-case-interview)
77. **Show ownership** — "Here's how I'd approach this for the client." [Leland — BCG guide](https://www.joinleland.com/library/a/breaking-down-bcg-interviews-what-to-expect-and-how-to-prepare)
78. **Mirror the interviewer's framing language** — if they said "profitability", don't switch to "earnings". [Management Consulted — Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)
79. **Pause silently before talking** — 5-10 sec of composed silence reads as confidence. [Casebasix — Structure thinking](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/structure-thinking-case-interview)
80. **Treat the recap as a handshake** — it builds rapport and signals listening. [Management Consulted — Recap](https://managementconsulted.com/case-interview-recap/)

### 1.3 What weak candidates do in the first 30 seconds (red flags)

81. **Jumping into structure before confirming the objective.** Signals "showing off" not problem-solving. [Caseinterviewhub — 8 mistakes](https://www.caseinterviewhub.com/blog/mistakes-case-preparation-mckinsey-bcg-bain)
82. **Asking 4+ clarifying questions** — reads as nervousness/stalling. [PrepLounge — How many clarifying questions](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/how-many-clarifying-questions-are-appropriate-and-when-should-they-be-asked-1869)
83. **Weak opener like "I'm not sure what this means"** — signals uncertainty, breaks credibility instantly. [Casebasix — Clarifying questions](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/clarifying-questions-case-interview)
84. **"So, um, can you tell me more?"** — same: low-confidence verbal pattern.
85. **Asking analysis-style questions up front** ("How much are revenues declining?") — those belong in the body. [PrepLounge — Clarifying](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/clarifying-questions-1786)
86. **Asking about competition/customer satisfaction up front** — should be inside framework, not pre-framework.
87. **Talking through think-time aloud** the whole time — looks unable to think internally. [PrepLounge — How long to think](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/how-long-should-you-be-taking-to-think-in-case-interviews-22402)
88. **Going silent for >3 minutes** — interviewers find it uncomfortable; talk through your logic if you must.
89. **Forgetting to verbalise process** — "silence makes you look stuck." [Hacking the Case Interview — McKinsey Style](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mckinsey-style-case-interview)
90. **Trying to "take over" a McKinsey case** as if it were Bain — looks uncoachable.
91. **Pausing repeatedly to ask "how am I doing?"** — explicitly named a "bad thing to do." [RocketBlocks — Don't be that candidate](https://www.rocketblocks.me/blog/that-candidate.php)
92. **Asking the interviewer "What about me would make a great consultant?"** — puts them on the spot. [RocketBlocks](https://www.rocketblocks.me/blog/that-candidate.php)
93. **Trying to interrogate or trick the interviewer** — reverses the dynamic, reads as desperate.
94. **Complaining about previous roles in fit chat** before the case — flags "complainer" persona. [RocketBlocks](https://www.rocketblocks.me/blog/that-candidate.php)
95. **Misinterpreting the scope** because they didn't recap. [Caseinterviewhub — 8 mistakes](https://www.caseinterviewhub.com/blog/mistakes-case-preparation-mckinsey-bcg-bain)
96. **Diving into a memorised framework verbatim** — interviewers spot it instantly and downgrade. [Casebasix — Frameworks](https://www.casebasix.com/pages/case-interview-frameworks-guide)
97. **Force-fitting a profitability tree onto a market-entry case** — explicit named anti-pattern.
98. **Rambling 30+ seconds before getting to the recap.** Communication = control; rambling = no control.
99. **Skipping the recap entirely.** Common; major red flag because interviewer can't tell if you understood.
100. **Hedging recap language** ("I think maybe the client wants…") — kill confidence.
101. **Asking interviewer for permission to take notes** mid-prompt — shows uncertainty about basics.
102. **Vague or hedging conclusions, mirrored at open** ("It depends…") — McKinsey explicitly penalises this.
103. **Asking questions whose answers don't change your structure** — wastes the budget.
104. **Burning >2 min on clarifications** — eats structure time.
105. **Repeating the prompt word-for-word** instead of paraphrasing — adds zero signal.
106. **Looking surprised by ambiguity** — interviewers expect ambiguity; surprise reads as inexperience.
107. **Asking what framework to use** — biggest tell of an unprepared candidate.
108. **Performing rehearsed cadence** ("step one… step two…") — flagged as "felt rehearsed and lacks creativity" feedback by McKinsey interviewers. [PrepLounge — Rehearsed feedback](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/i-have-recently-received-my-feedback-on-a-case-interview-the-interviewer-mentioned-that-my-performance-in-the-case-was-great-however-it-felt-rehearsed-and-lacks-creativity-what-does-it-mean-that-it-fe-21841)
109. **Trying to confirm the framework with the interviewer** ("Does that make sense?") repeatedly — reads as needing approval.
110. **Showing visible panic / writing then crossing out** — readable through silence; tank impression.

## 2. Clarification phase

### 2.1 Questions interviewers EXPECT (and reward)

111. **"How does the company actually make money?"** — business-model question; #1 expected. [Hacking the Case Interview — Clarifying](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/clarifying-questions-to-ask-in-a-case-interview)
112. **"What does success look like — revenue, profit, share?"** — objective metric; rewarded because it focuses everything downstream.
113. **"Over what time horizon?"** — short vs long-term lens changes the entire framework.
114. **"Are there any constraints — budget, regulatory, M&A on/off the table?"** — surfaces unstated limits.
115. **"What products or services do they sell?"** — basic product clarification.
116. **"Who are their customers — B2B, B2C, both?"** — channel/customer-base clarifier.
117. **"Direct to consumer or through retailers/partners?"** — distribution structure.
118. **"One geography or multiple?"** — geography scope clarifier; rewarded because it's MECE-relevant.
119. **"What does 'widget' [or any jargon] mean here?"** — Cosentino: never lose points for asking about jargon. [Cosentino, *Case in Point*](https://caseinterview.com/case-in-point)
120. **"Is the decline upstream, midstream, or downstream?"** — segmentation question for revenue cases.
121. **"Are we entering organically or via acquisition?"** — market-entry clarifier; rewarded because it changes the framework branches.
122. **"Public or private company?"** — affects financing/optionality.
123. **"Who is asking — the CEO, the COO, a PE owner?"** — decision-maker identification.
124. **"Is this an industry-wide problem or company-specific?"** — narrows to internal vs external root causes.
125. **"How long has this been happening?"** — temporal scope; rewarded because it informs hypothesis.
126. **"Just to confirm — the goal is X, and we're focused on Y. Right?"** — combined recap-as-clarification.
127. **"What's the ask in one sentence?"** — sharp candidate move.
128. **"Is there a target number we're trying to hit?"** — targets quantitative success criterion.
129. **"Why now — is there a trigger event?"** — surface pressure source.
130. **"What has the client already tried?"** — prevents you proposing things they've discarded.
131. **"Is the company profitable today?"** — context-anchor for any growth/strategy case.

### 2.2 Clarifying questions interviewers PUSHBACK on

132. **"How big is the market?"** — analysis question disguised as clarification; should appear inside structure.
133. **"How much have revenues declined?"** — explicitly named bad up-front question.
134. **"Who are the competitors?"** — body-of-case question, not clarification.
135. **"What are current growth trends?"** — same — analysis, not clarification.
136. **"How is customer satisfaction?"** — same.
137. **"What's their cost structure?"** — analysis bucket, not clarification.
138. **"What framework should I use?"** — biggest single tell of an unprepared candidate.
139. **"Can you repeat the prompt?" (after 2 min)** — interviewers note inattention.
140. **"Is there anything else I should know?"** — open-ended fishing question; usually answered "no" curtly.
141. **Asking 4+ clarifications back-to-back.** Reads as stalling; interviewers cap engagement.
142. **"What does 'strategy' mean here?"** — too abstract; interviewers won't define their terms.
143. **"Can you tell me more about the industry?"** — too broad; should be inside framework.
144. **"What's the answer you're looking for?"** — fatal — interpreted as fishing.
145. **Multiple yes/no questions with no underlying logic** — feels like checklist mode.
146. **"Should I use a framework?"** — never ask; just deliver one.

### 2.3 Exact phrases interviewers use to redirect

147. **"Let's set that aside for now."** — neutral park signal.
148. **"Let's come back to that later."** — soft redirect, common at Bain.
149. **"Let's not go there yet."** — direct redirect when candidate jumps ahead.
150. **"For now, focus on [X]."** — hard narrowing.
151. **"I want to push you on something else."** — McKinsey-style topic switch.
152. **"Let's move on."** — explicit pace signal; means "you've spent enough here."
153. **"Why don't we focus on [X] for now?"** — softer Bain variant.
154. **"Park that for now and let's come back."** — explicit park language.
155. **"Good question, but for now assume [X]."** — common deflection where interviewer hands you an assumption.
156. **"Okay, great answer — now I'd like to switch gears."** — interviewer-led abrupt-switch standard at McKinsey.
157. **"Let's say [X] is true for now."** — assumption-handoff to keep candidate moving.
158. **"For the purpose of this case, assume Y."** — hard assumption injection.
159. **"That's a different question — let's tackle it later."** — explicit topical postponement.
160. **"What would you recommend based on what you have so far?"** — pace-compression signal that time is short.
161. **"Oh, that's so very interesting…"** — flagged transitional language; interviewer is buying time, not endorsing.

## 3. Structure / Framework phase

### 3.1 What "MECE" looks like at MBB (with examples)

162. **MECE = mutually exclusive AND collectively exhaustive.** Buckets don't overlap; together they cover the whole problem. [Hacking the Case Interview — MECE](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mece)
163. **Profitability tree as MECE example:** Revenue − Cost. Then Revenue = Price × Volume; Cost = Fixed + Variable. Each split is clean. [IGotAnOffer — MECE](https://igotanoffer.com/blogs/mckinsey-case-interview-blog/mece)
164. **NOT MECE: "Price, Volume, Revenue"** — Revenue overlaps with Price × Volume. Classic interviewer trap. [NextEpMBB — MECE](https://nextepmbb.com/en/mece-framework-case-interviews-en/)
165. **NOT MECE customer segmentation: "Children, Teenagers, Adults, People under 30"** — overlap in last category.
166. **NOT MECE: "Marketing" branch and "Revenue Growth" branch** — marketing initiatives sit in both; double-count risk.
167. **NOT MECE: "Millennials" and "Online shoppers"** — many millennials shop online; overlap.
168. **Restaurant revenue case anti-pattern:** Marketing & Brand Perception in same framework — Customer Experience feeds Brand Perception so they overlap; classic interviewer probe: "Which bucket does an ad campaign communicating bad customer experience fit into?" [NextEpMBB — MECE](https://nextepmbb.com/en/mece-framework-case-interviews-en/)
169. **MECE applies at every layer of the issue tree, not just the top.** [MConsultingPrep — MECE 6 rules](https://mconsultingprep.com/case-interview-mece)
170. **Stress-test MECE by asking "where does X fit?"** for any plausible factor.
171. **Standard MECE splits MBB candidates use:** internal/external; supply/demand; quantitative/qualitative; financial/strategic; revenue/cost; short-term/long-term.
172. **It's easier to check overlaps than gaps** — quick-scan buckets for bleed before presenting.
173. **3-4 top-level buckets is the sweet spot.** Fewer = shallow, more = unmemorable.
174. **Buckets must be tied to the case-specific objective**, not generic.
175. **The "what would have to be true?" test:** ask yourself "what 3-4 things must be true for me to confidently recommend?" — those become the buckets.
176. **Each bucket should be hypothesis-loaded** — name the question it answers, not just the topic.
177. **Turn paper toward interviewer when presenting** — physical handoff is part of the MECE delivery.

### 3.2 Phrases interviewers use when structure is weak

178. **"How are these mutually exclusive?"** — direct MECE challenge.
179. **"I see some overlap there."** — softer MECE flag.
180. **"Walk me through how those buckets are different."** — pushes candidate to defend MECE.
181. **"Where would [X] fit in your structure?"** — classic stress-test; restaurant ad-campaign example.
182. **"Does anything not fit into these buckets?"** — exhaustiveness probe.
183. **"Why these three?"** — forces candidate to articulate MECE rationale.
184. **"That feels generic."** — direct call-out of a memorised framework.
185. **"That sounds rehearsed."** — verbatim feedback documented from McKinsey interviewers. [PrepLounge — Rehearsed feedback](https://www.preplounge.com/consulting-forum/i-have-recently-received-my-feedback-on-a-case-interview-the-interviewer-mentioned-that-my-performance-in-the-case-was-great-however-it-felt-rehearsed-and-lacks-creativity-what-does-it-mean-that-it-fe-21841)
186. **"Tell me why that's relevant here."** — challenges generic 4Ps/5Cs application.
187. **"That's a 4-P framework — what does it actually tell us about this client?"** — direct anti-canned-framework hit.
188. **"What's missing?"** — exhaustiveness probe.
189. **"Aren't [X] and [Y] really the same thing?"** — overlap probe.
190. **"You haven't covered [X]."** — gap call-out.
191. **"Which of these matters most for the client?"** — prioritisation push when buckets are flat.
192. **"Where would you start?"** — same as above; forces hypothesis.
193. **"Why this order?"** — challenges candidate's prioritisation logic.
194. **"What's your hypothesis?"** — most common follow-up to a flat structure.
195. **"Is that the most important driver?"** — challenge to first-bucket selection.
196. **"Can you go one level deeper?"** — depth push.
197. **"Break that bucket down for me."** — forces sub-trees.
198. **"What does that bucket actually mean?"** — challenges vague label.
199. **"That sounds like the textbook answer."** — overt anti-rote signal.
200. **"How does that connect to [client objective]?"** — forces objective alignment.
201. **"You're describing a generic profitability tree — make it specific to this client."** — explicit anti-rote at MBB.
202. **"Are these collectively exhaustive?"** — direct CE probe.
203. **"What if [factor] doesn't fall in any of these?"** — exhaustiveness probe.
204. **"You've given me categories — what's the analysis you'd do under each?"** — pushes from labels to actions.
205. **"That's not really a structure, that's a list."** — sharp flag for non-tree thinking.
206. **"You've covered the same idea twice."** — overlap call-out.
207. **"How is this different from a generic 5-Cs?"** — anti-rote probe.

### 3.3 Phrases interviewers use when structure is strong

208. **"Great. Let's dig into [bucket X]."** — quick acceptance, immediate dive.
209. **"Good — where would you start?"** — accepts structure, demands prioritisation.
210. **"That's helpful."** — neutral-positive, conversational at Bain.
211. **Pace acceleration** — interviewer hands data without asking — implicit "your structure is fine, here's the data."
212. **"Why don't we start there."** — approval-via-action.
213. **"That's a clear structure."** — explicit (rare) approval.
214. **"Once you have ensured the interviewer's buy-in to your structure, you are more than halfway through the case."** — verbatim from MyConsultingCoach on what acceptance signals. [MyConsultingCoach](https://www.myconsultingcoach.com/case-interview)
215. **"Sounds good — what's your hypothesis?"** — accepts structure, immediately tests next layer.
216. **Lack of pushback / minimal probing** — itself a signal of acceptance; interviewers conserve probes for weak structures.
217. **"OK." (short, then handing over data)** — micro-acceptance; interviewer-led shorthand.
218. **"Let's go with that."** — clean approval.
219. **"Take me through bucket one."** — implicit acceptance; immediately consume the structure.
220. **"Why that one first?"** — accepts structure, tests prioritisation logic (not a knock).
221. **"I like the logic."** — explicit (rare) approval; usually paired with a probe.
222. **Interviewer starts handing exhibits** without challenging buckets — strongest non-verbal acceptance.

### 3.4 Time pressure tactics interviewers use

223. **"What would you recommend based on what you have so far?"** — explicit pace-compression. Means: synthesise now.
224. **"Let's move on."** — pace signal; means stop here.
225. **Skipping branches** of your framework explicitly: "Let's skip [X] and go to [Y]."
226. **Mini-case format**: 5-15 min instead of 30-45, compressing structure + math + recommendation. [Hacking the Case Interview — Mini case](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mini-case-interview)
227. **Abrupt switch:** "Okay, great answer — now I want to ask you about something different." Standard McKinsey interviewer-led move.
228. **Withholding data unless asked** — forces candidate to prioritise.
229. **Setting an explicit timer:** "We have 2 minutes for this part." — common in BCG written/PEI hybrid rounds.
230. **Pace-test by asking for a recommendation early:** "If I had to walk into the boardroom in 30 seconds, what would you tell me?"
231. **Forcing assumption injection:** "Assume X is true and move on." — strips candidate of analysis time.
232. **"Interviewers consistently say they would rather see a candidate make a quick call with limited data than watch them spin their wheels."** — verbatim from compressed-time guidance. [Hacking the Case Interview — Mini case](https://www.hackingthecaseinterview.com/pages/mini-case-interview)
233. **Hard cut-off mid-thought:** "I'm going to stop you there." — pace signal that you've drifted.
